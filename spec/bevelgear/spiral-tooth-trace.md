# Drawing the spiral-bevel tooth trace — a Euclidean construction

This describes, as a straightedge-and-compass construction, how a single spiral-bevel
**tooth trace** is drawn: the curved centreline that one tooth follows across the cone
face. It is the *genuine* circular-arc (Gleason face-mill) trace — a real circle of the
cutter's radius — **not** a point-sampled / fitted spline. Everything here happens in a
single flat plane (the cone's tangent plane); the curve is never laid onto the cone as
geometry — its effect on the tooth is applied analytically as a twist about the shaft axis
(§8). Lengths are cone distances; angles are real angles in that plane.

The point of writing it out is verification: each numbered step below should correspond to
one piece of the construction in the spiral branch of `lib/geargen/bevelgear.py` (§3a of
`instructions.md`). If a step has no counterpart, or the
code does something the step does not justify, that is the bug.

---

## 0. What the trace is, physically

A spiral-bevel tooth is cut by a circular face-mill cutter of radius `r_c`. As the cutter
sweeps, the point where it touches the cone face traces a **circular arc of radius `r_c`**.
That arc, lying on the cone, is the tooth trace. So the trace is, exactly, *an arc of a
circle whose radius is the cutter radius* — full stop. The only questions are **where its
centre sits** and **which portion of it** we keep.

A straight bevel is the special case `r_c → ∞` (the arc becomes the straight cone element).

---

## 1. The drawing plane (the cone's tangent plane)

Pick the cone the trace lives on (here: the **root cone**, the cone generated by the
dedendum element `Apex→D` for the driving gear, `Apex→C` for the pinion). Take the **plane
tangent to that cone along that one element** — i.e. the flat plane that touches the cone
exactly along the line `Apex→D` and nowhere crosses into it. The whole construction is done
in this plane; think of it as a sheet of paper laid against the cone and touching it along
the cone element.

> **Why this plane is the right one (and a caveat).** In the textbook construction this flat
> plane is the plane of the **generating crown gear** — the flat reference gear that plays
> the role the rack plays for spur gears. Its plane is tangent to the **pitch cone** along
> the cone element, its centre is the apex, and the face-mill cutter sweeps its circular arc
> *in this plane*. So "draw the arc in the cone's tangent plane" is not an approximation —
> it is literally where the cutter cuts. **Caveat:** the canonical reference cone is the
> **pitch** cone; this implementation lays the trace on the **root** cone (`Apex→D`/`Apex→C`)
> instead. The two tangent planes differ only by the small dedendum angle, so the arc's shape
> is essentially identical and using the root cone is a defensible convenience for guiding
> the lengthwise tooth curve — but it is a departure from the canonical pitch-cone/crown-gear
> reference, and ψ then ends up measured on the root cone rather than the pitch cone.

Set up coordinates **in this plane**, with the **apex as the origin**:

- **x-axis** = the cone element itself, `Apex→D`, pointing *away from the apex toward the
  heel*. A point's x-coordinate is therefore its **cone distance R** (distance from the
  apex measured along the cone).
- **y-axis** = perpendicular to x **within the tangent plane** — this is the
  *circumferential* direction (the direction the tooth is displaced sideways from the
  straight radial element). Construct it as `axis × coneVec` so it is genuinely
  perpendicular to the element and lies in the tangent plane.

> Check: the apex is the origin, the x-axis is the actual `Apex→D` element (not `Apex→Apex2`
> and not the shaft axis), and +y is one consistent circumferential sense.

---

## 2. The three cone-distance marks

Along the x-axis mark three distances from the apex:

- `R_toe` — cone distance of the **toe** (the small/front end of the face), = distance from
  the apex to the toe edge midpoint, measured along the element.
- `R_heel` — cone distance of the **heel** (the large/back end), likewise.
- `R_mean = ½(R_toe + R_heel)` — the **mean** cone distance, midway along the face.

Call `M` the point `(R_mean, 0)` on the x-axis — the trace's reference point at mid-face.
The face width is `FW = R_heel − R_toe`.

---

## 3. The apex circles (loci of constant cone distance)

Every point at cone distance `R` lies, in this plane, on the **circle of radius `R` centred
at the apex (the origin)**. Draw three such circles: radius `R_toe`, `R_mean`, `R_heel`.
These are the "toe circle", "mean circle", "heel circle". The trace must start on the toe
circle and end on the heel circle. (They are concentric about the apex; do **not** centre
them anywhere else — that is the most natural place to get it wrong.)

---

## 4. The spiral angle and the hand

The **mean spiral angle ψ** is, by definition, the angle **between the tooth trace and the
cone element, measured at the mean point `M`**. Equivalently: the angle between the trace's
tangent at `M` and the x-axis. ψ = 0 ⇒ straight bevel.

> Use the **element**. The mainstream definition (AGMA/ISO usage, Wikipedia, KHK) measures ψ
> against the cone element (generator). A few looser sources phrase it against the cone
> *axis* or against the *perpendicular* to the element — those are imprecise (and 90° off);
> don't follow them. ψ is also, strictly, the *mean* spiral angle: it is only exactly ψ at
> `M`; toe and heel see different (larger/smaller) spiral angles.

The **hand** (left/right) chooses which way the trace leans off the radial line — i.e. the
sign of that lean. A meshing pair has **opposite hands**, so the pinion's construction uses
the opposite sign to the driving gear's. Encode the hand as `s = +1` or `s = −1`.

---

## 5. Locating the cutter-circle centre `C` (the crux)

The trace is an arc of radius `r_c` passing through `M` and making angle ψ with the x-axis
**at `M`**. A circle's tangent is perpendicular to its radius, so the centre `C` lies on the
line through `M` that is **perpendicular to the desired tangent at `M`**, at distance `r_c`
from `M`.

Construct it directly:

1. At `M`, the trace's tangent makes angle ψ with the **x-axis** (the element).
2. Therefore the radius `M→C` makes angle ψ with the **y-axis** (it is perpendicular to the
   tangent). Its **along-element** component always points toward the apex (`−sin ψ`); the
   **hand** chooses which side of the cone element the centre sits on (`±cos ψ`). So `M→C`
   points in the direction `(−sin ψ, s·cos ψ)`.
3. Step a distance `r_c` from `M` along that direction. So, in plane coordinates:

   ```
   C = ( R_mean − r_c · sin ψ ,  s · r_c · cos ψ )
   ```

   (`|M→C| = r_c` automatically, since (r_c sinψ)² + (r_c cosψ)² = r_c².)

   ⚠️ The hand sign `s` belongs on the **cos (y)** term, not the sin (x) term. Opposite
   hands are mirror images **across the cone element (y=0)**, which flips `Cy`. Putting `s`
   on `Cx` instead mirrors across `x=R_mean`, which is a *different* curve and gives the two
   gears unequal twist — a real bug to watch for.

That is the **entire** dependence on ψ, the hand, and `r_c`. The cutter circle is the circle
of radius `r_c` centred at `C`; by construction it passes through `M` and is tangent there to
a line at angle ψ to the element.

> Checks:
> - `C` is exactly `r_c` from `M` (so the cutter circle passes through `M`).
> - Flipping the hand `s` mirrors `C` across the x-axis-through-`M` radial — the two hands
>   are mirror images, nothing else changes. For equal-tooth gears the driving and pinion
>   traces must come out as exact mirror images.
> - With ψ = 0, `C = (R_mean, r_c)` is straight "north" of `M`; the arc through `M` is then
>   tangent to the element ⇒ straight tooth in the limit.

---

## 6. Toe and heel ends (circle–circle intersections)

The trace runs from the toe circle to the heel circle, **along the cutter circle**:

- **Toe point** `T` = intersection of the **cutter circle** (centre `C`, radius `r_c`) with
  the **toe circle** (centre apex, radius `R_toe`).
- **Heel point** `H` = intersection of the cutter circle with the **heel circle**
  (radius `R_heel`).

Each circle pair meets in two points; take the one **on the working side** — the same branch
that `M` sits on (the near intersection, the one continuously reachable from `M` along the
cutter circle without going around the far side). Practically: of the two, pick the
intersection closer to `M` / on the same side of the line `apex→C`.

To make the kept arc reach cleanly past the conical end-trims, the ends may be taken a
hair beyond the face — intersect with circles of radius `R_toe − ε` and `R_heel + ε`
(small ε, a few % of `FW`) instead of exactly `R_toe`, `R_heel`.

> Check: `T` is at cone distance `R_toe` (it lies on the toe circle) and `H` at `R_heel`.
> If a "toe" point comes out at the wrong radius, the wrong intersection branch was kept.

---

## 7. The trace arc

The tooth trace is the **arc of the cutter circle from `T` through `M` to `H`** — one single
circular arc, radius `r_c`, centre `C`. Drawn as a 3-point arc it is the arc through
`T`, `M`, `H`; constrain its centre coincident with `C` and its radius equal to `r_c` so it
is the genuine cutter circle and not a look-alike spline.

Note the spiral angle is exactly ψ **only at `M`**; toward toe and heel it drifts (it is
larger at the toe, smaller at the heel, because a fixed circle meets the ever-larger apex
circles at changing angles). That drift is correct and is the signature of a real
circular-cut spiral bevel — not an error.

---

## 8. From the flat trace to the tooth — the analytic twist (no projection)

The arc above is **never laid onto the cone as 3-D geometry**. Earlier versions projected the
flat arc onto the root cone (`projectToSurface` along the tangent-plane normal) and measured
the trace azimuth on the cone; that approach is **removed** — for unequal-ratio pairs the arc
wraps around the cone and comes back as multiple disjoint fragments, so the measured azimuth
collapses to a fraction of the true sweep (see `instructions.md` §3a step D, which forbids
reintroducing it). Instead the tooth's rotation about the gear **shaft axis** is computed
**analytically** from the flat 2-D arc endpoints (`instructions.md` §3a step G):

- `phi_crown = atan2(heel_y, heel_x) − atan2(toe_y, toe_x)` — the angle the arc's toe and
  heel endpoints subtend **at the apex** in the flat frame (the developed crown-gear plane);
- `total = |phi_crown| / sin γ` — the toe→heel twist magnitude about the shaft axis, with γ
  this gear's **pitch** cone angle; the hand sign sets the direction.

The straight tooth is then sliced into cross-section slabs and each slab is rotated about the
shaft axis by its linear share of `total`, centred on `R_mean` so the mid-face section stays
unrotated. The flat trace sketch itself remains reference-only geometry.

> **Why `1/sin γ` — and where it goes wrong.** On the developed (unrolled) cone, an in-plane
> swept angle `φ` corresponds to a rotation about the shaft axis of **`θ = φ / sin γ`**
> (γ = pitch angle); a full turn about the axis, θ = 2π, develops to an arc of only
> 2π·sin γ. So the toe→heel *shaft-axis* twist is **not** the angle the flat arc subtends at
> the apex — it is that angle divided by `sin γ`. A correct-looking trace can still give a
> wrong tooth if this `1/sin γ` factor is mishandled: use the **pitch** cone angle, not the
> root-cone angle (`acos(coneVec·axisDir)` measures the root cone and inflates the twist),
> and expect the two members of a pair to get legitimately **different** twists (same ψ and
> cutter, different γ). This is the most likely culprit when the trace looks right but a
> pair won't mesh.

---

## 9. Invariants to verify the implementation against

A correct trace must satisfy all of:

1. **Apex-centred.** Toe/mean/heel loci are circles about the apex; `R` is distance from the
   apex along `Apex→D`.
2. **Cutter radius.** The arc's radius equals `r_c` everywhere (it is one circle).
3. **Passes through `M`.** The arc contains `(R_mean, 0)`; the centre `C` is `r_c` from `M`.
4. **Spiral angle at the mean.** The angle between the arc's tangent at `M` and the element
   is ψ.
5. **Mirror symmetry.** Swapping the hand reflects the whole construction across the radial
   through `M` and changes nothing else; an equal-tooth pair's two traces are mirror images.
6. **Ends on the right circles.** `T` on the toe circle, `H` on the heel circle, both on the
   `M` side branch.
7. **Straight-bevel limit.** ψ → 0 (or `r_c` → ∞) returns the straight cone element.

If the rendered trace fails any of these — especially (1) wrong centre for the apex circles,
(4) ψ not actually realised at `M`, or (5) the two gears not mirror images — that is where to
look.

---

## 10. Common ways to draw it wrong (debugging checklist)

- **Tangent plane on the wrong element.** Built along `Apex→Apex2` or the shaft axis instead
  of `Apex→D`/`Apex→C`; the whole frame is then skewed.
- **Apex circles not centred on the apex.** Using the back-cone centre or a sketch origin
  makes `R` not a true cone distance, distorting toe/heel placement.
- **ψ applied about the wrong point.** ψ is the trace-vs-element angle *at `M`*; if it is fed
  in as a rotation about the section centre, or about the shaft axis, the curvature is wrong.
- **Hand applied inconsistently.** The hand sign must flip for the pinion *and* nothing else
  may differ for equal teeth; if the pinion comes out more/less curved than the driving, the
  asymmetry is upstream (e.g. `R_mean`, `r_c`, or ψ differing between the two).
- **Kept the wrong intersection branch** for `T` or `H` (far side of the cutter circle),
  giving a kinked or back-bent trace.
- **Replaced the arc with a fitted spline** through sampled points — looks similar, is not
  the cutter circle, and will not carry the radius/centre constraints.
